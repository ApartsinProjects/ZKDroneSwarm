6. Experiments

This section describes the experimental configuration used to evaluate the methods presented in Section 4. It specifies the benchmark scenario, training protocol, evaluation procedure, and the metrics used to compare policies. The goal of the experimental design is to instantiate the ZK-MRTA problem definition from Section 3 in a concrete, reproducible setting, and to evaluate the three policies — random, oracle, and decentralized matrix factorization — under identical conditions.

The experimental design is structured around the four dimensions identified in the research question: the latent compatibility structure of the environment, the exploration strategy of the learning policy, the supervision mode used for online updates, and the swarm composition. Each dimension is described in the subsections below, along with the specific parameter choices made for this evaluation.

6.1 Benchmark Scenario

The evaluation uses a single benchmark scenario generated by the ZK-MRTA framework described in Section 5. The scenario instantiates a swarm of $m = 9$ drones operating in a discrete two-dimensional world of size $1000 \times 1000$ units, with a task set of $n = 27$ targets. This produces a 3:1 target-to-drone ratio, requiring sustained and coordinated engagement rather than one-shot assignment.

The latent compatibility structure is governed by a shared latent space of dimension $d = 3$, corresponding to three latent compatibility modes. Drone and target latent vectors are generated independently under the independent mode, meaning drone mode centers and target mode centers are drawn from separate families. Each mode center is constructed using the one-hot center_mode, which places centers along the coordinate axes, producing an axis-aligned orthogonal latent structure. Individual drone and target vectors are then sampled around their respective mode centers with Gaussian variance $\sigma^2 = 0.04$ (standard deviation $0.2$) for both populations. Under this configuration, the compatibility space exhibits a soft block-diagonal structure: drones and targets in the same latent mode are expected to interact more effectively, but the variance around mode centers ensures that the structure is imperfect and cannot be recovered by simple rules.

Each target is initialized with a health budget of $h = 10.0$ HP. Damage per interaction is determined by the latent dot product between drone and target vectors, clipped to zero for negative values. Targets become inactive when their HP reaches zero. An episode terminates either when all 27 targets are neutralized or when $T = 250$ steps have elapsed, whichever occurs first.

The observation channel is subject to reward noise and observation noise, both set to $\sigma_{\text{noise}} = 0.2$. Reward noise perturbs the scalar reward signal received after each interaction; observation noise corrupts the observed action identities of other agents in the swarm-history component of the observation tuple. Both noise sources are designed to stress-test the policy's ability to learn from imperfect interaction feedback under the zero-knowledge constraint.

The no-operation action is disabled (allow_noop = false): agents must select an active target at every step, removing the option to abstain from engagement.

6.2 Swarm Composition

The swarm composition is defined by the joint configuration of drone count, target count, and their ratio. This evaluation uses $m = 9$ drones and $n = 27$ targets, yielding a ratio of 3:1. Given the latent structure of three modes, this composition produces an average of 3 drones per mode and 9 targets per mode in expectation. The swarm is therefore large enough to create non-trivial coordination dynamics — multiple drones compete for the same targets at each step — but not so large as to make target exhaustion trivially fast.

This composition is a fixed experimental condition in the current evaluation. Variations in drone count, target count, and their ratio are identified as a direction for future investigation in Section 8.

6.3 Exploration Strategy

The decentralized matrix-factorization policy uses an $\varepsilon$-greedy exploration mechanism with multiplicative decay. The initial exploration rate is set to $\varepsilon_0 = 0.3$, which ensures that a substantial fraction of early interactions are exploratory and contribute to coverage of the drone-target interaction space. At each decision step, the exploration rate decays by a factor of $\delta = 0.9995$, producing a smooth and gradual transition from exploration to exploitation. A minimum floor of $\varepsilon_{\min} = 0.02$ prevents full exploitation and preserves residual exploration throughout training.

Under these settings, the exploration rate decays from approximately $0.27$ at the end of episode 1 to $0.052$ at the end of episode 35. The floor of $0.02$ was not reached within the training horizon, indicating that the policy remained in active exploration decay throughout the experiment. This is relevant context for interpreting training dynamics in Section 7.

The random and oracle policies do not use an exploration mechanism. The random baseline samples uniformly from available targets at every step, and the oracle selects greedily from its privileged model. Neither policy undergoes learning or epsilon decay.

6.4 Supervision Mode

The matrix-factorization policy is trained using the integration-matrix supervision mode described in §4.5.1. Under this mode, the policy maintains a running-mean interaction matrix accumulated from observed engagement events. The integration matrix serves as the supervision target for online gradient updates: predicted utilities are regressed against accumulated interaction estimates rather than raw immediate rewards. Crucially, the engagement-validity mask $\mathbf{w}_{t-1}$ acts as an eligibility gate — events targeting already-inactive tasks are excluded entirely from both accumulation and gradient updates. This prevents dead-target signals from distorting the learned compatibility estimates under noisy conditions.

The factorization dimension of the policy's internal model is $d_f = 3$, matching the environment's latent dimension $d = 3$. This alignment means the policy has, in principle, sufficient representational capacity to recover the true compatibility structure, though it has no access to the true latent vectors and must infer the structure from scratch through observed interaction outcomes.

Learning parameters are set as follows: learning rate $\eta = 0.01$, $L_2$ regularization coefficient $\lambda = 0.02$. The embeddings are initialized from $\mathcal{N}(0, 0.01^2)$. Learning is persistent across episodes: the policy's embedding matrices are carried forward at episode boundaries rather than re-initialized, so knowledge accumulated in earlier episodes continues to shape future decisions.

The direct supervision mode — in which the policy regresses directly against immediate rewards rather than accumulated integration estimates — is not evaluated in the current experiments. Comparison of the two supervision modes is noted as a direction for future work in Section 8.

6.5 Evaluation Protocol

The matrix-factorization policy is trained over 35 consecutive episodes on the same benchmark instance. The random and oracle baselines are each evaluated on a single episode of the same scenario. All three policies operate under identical observation constraints, action spaces, and termination conditions.

This design reflects the cumulative nature of the learning-based policy: because embeddings are not reset between episodes, performance should improve over the training horizon as the policy accumulates evidence about the hidden compatibility structure. Evaluation is therefore conducted at the episode level, and performance is tracked across all 35 episodes to allow training dynamics to be examined alongside final-episode results.

The following metrics are computed for each episode and used to compare policies:

  • Steps: Number of timesteps from episode start to termination. Lower is better.
  • Total ammo used: Total shots fired by all agents. Lower is better.
  • Shots per target: Total ammo divided by targets neutralized. Lower is better and measures average engagement efficiency.
  • Targets neutralized: Count of targets reduced to zero HP. The maximum is 27. Higher is better.
  • Success: Binary indicator of whether all 27 targets were neutralized before the step limit.
  • Total overkill: Cumulative wasted damage dealt to targets already inactive. Lower is better.
  • Total collisions: Number of timesteps in which multiple agents selected the same active target simultaneously.
  • Average latent match quality: The fraction of optimal damage achieved per shot, defined as total gross damage divided by the sum of optimal damage over all shots fired. A value of 1.0 means every shot was fired by the optimally matched drone for that target; lower values indicate suboptimal pairing. Higher is better.
  • Total latent mismatch: Cumulative shortfall in damage due to suboptimal drone-target pairing. For each shot at an active target, this is the difference between the best possible damage any drone could have inflicted on that target and the actual damage dealt. Lower is better.
  • Mean reward per agent: Average total reward accumulated per drone over the episode.

Policy comparison is structured as follows. The oracle benchmark defines an upper bound. The random baseline defines a lower bound. The matrix-factorization policy is evaluated against both: its performance is reported relative to the random baseline to assess the gain from learning, and relative to the oracle to assess the gap from the idealized ceiling. For learning-curve analysis, performance is tracked across all 35 training episodes of the matrix-factorization policy; for direct cross-policy comparison, the best episode of the matrix-factorization policy (episode 35) is compared to the single baseline episodes.
