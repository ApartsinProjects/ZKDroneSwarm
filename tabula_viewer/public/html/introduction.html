1. Introduction

Swarm robotics has emerged as a compelling framework for coordinating large numbers of autonomous agents in dynamic, uncertain, and high-risk environments. Advances in aerial, ground, and maritime autonomous systems have made it increasingly feasible to deploy heterogeneous swarms whose members differ in sensing, payload, endurance, or strike capability. A representative example is a drone swarm operating in a contested environment, where agents must repeatedly decide which targets to engage while the mission unfolds. Analogous allocation challenges arise in civilian domains: assigning maintenance crews with unknown specializations to equipment faults of unknown type, routing service requests to workers in a distributed workforce platform, or scheduling heterogeneous compute jobs across machines with uncharacterized performance profiles. In all these settings, the effectiveness of the system depends not only on individual agent performance, but also on whether tasks are allocated in a way that avoids redundancy, adapts to uncertainty, and makes productive use of the agents' diversity.

This challenge falls under the broader problem of Multi-Robot Task Allocation (MRTA), which studies how tasks should be assigned across multiple agents to optimize overall system performance. Classical MRTA approaches include optimization-based methods, market-based mechanisms, reinforcement learning, and coalition-formation strategies. Although these approaches differ in mechanism, they typically rely on some combination of prior knowledge about tasks, prior knowledge about agent capabilities, and explicit inter-agent communication. These assumptions are often reasonable in structured settings, but they become restrictive in environments where task properties are hidden, capabilities are not known in advance, and coordination must emerge under severe informational constraints.

This work addresses that gap by introducing Zero-Knowledge Multi-Robot Task Allocation (ZK-MRTA), a problem formulation in which agents must allocate tasks without prior knowledge of task attributes, without knowledge of their own or others' capabilities, and without direct communication. Under this formulation, agents may learn only from the outcomes of their own actions and from limited public evidence generated by the swarm. More precisely, the environment may contain a true hidden compatibility structure between drones and targets, but that structure is never directly exposed to the agents. Instead, agents observe only restricted world-state information and public interaction traces, and must act without access to explicit symbolic matching rules.

The novelty of this research therefore lies not only in proposing a new solution method, but in defining a new task-allocation setting whose assumptions differ fundamentally from those of standard MRTA. Rather than assuming that costs, feasibility, or compatibility are known or can be communicated, ZK-MRTA treats them as latent quantities that must be inferred from sparse experience over time. This also changes how recommendation-inspired ideas should be understood in the project: the goal is not preference prediction, but latent utility estimation under sequential, decentralized, and observation-limited conditions. Accordingly, the collaborative-filtering perspective is best viewed here as a decentralized local matrix-factorization-inspired framing for hidden drone-target effectiveness, rather than as a centralized recommendation engine. Within this framing, the specific matrix factorization mechanism admits multiple variants—differing in how interaction evidence is accumulated and used for supervision, what exploration strategy governs action selection, and how negative signals are weighted—whose relative merits become an empirical question. Evaluating these variants requires strategy-independent performance metrics that capture both task completion effectiveness and coordination efficiency, such as damage efficiency, resource utilization, and coordination overhead.

This motivates the following research question: To what extent can a decentralized, collaborative-filtering-inspired approach optimize swarm performance under strict Zero-Knowledge constraints, and how do structural parameters — including latent compatibility structure, exploration strategy, supervision mode, and swarm composition — affect the convergence and coordination efficiency of the learned policy?

This paper makes the following contributions. First, it formalizes Zero-Knowledge Multi-Robot Task Allocation as a distinct task-allocation setting in which compatibility, capability, and task structure are hidden from the agents and cannot be recovered through direct communication, together with a suite of strategy-independent performance metrics covering task effectiveness, resource utilization, and coordination efficiency. Second, it introduces a family of decentralized collaborative-filtering-inspired matrix-factorization policies that learn latent drone-target utility from sparse public interaction outcomes, encompassing variants that differ in supervision mode, exploration mechanism, and signal weighting. Third, it provides a modular benchmark environment and experimentation framework, built on Gymnasium and PettingZoo, featuring a plug-in policy architecture for systematic comparison of strategies, structured episode logging and analysis reporting, and a visualization front-end for inspecting latent learning dynamics. Fourth, it presents an empirical exploration of how key experimental parameters—including latent dimension, observation noise, exploration schedule, and swarm composition—affect convergence behavior, coordination efficiency, and task completion across strategies.
