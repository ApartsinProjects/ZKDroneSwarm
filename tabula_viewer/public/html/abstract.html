Abstract

Zero-Knowledge Multi-Robot Task Allocation (ZK-MRTA) is a decentralized task-allocation setting in which agents have no prior knowledge of task attributes, no knowledge of their own or others' capabilities, and no direct communication channel. Under these constraints, agents must learn from the outcomes of interaction rather than from explicit models of compatibility, feasibility, or cost. This paper formalizes ZK-MRTA as a distinct MRTA problem class and presents a decentralized matrix-factorization policy that reinterprets collaborative filtering as online latent-utility learning: each drone maintains a private local model of drone-target effectiveness, selects actions from its own utility estimates, and updates that model from shared observations of engagement outcomes without parameter sharing.

To evaluate this setting, the paper introduces a Gymnasium-compatible PettingZoo benchmark that enforces zero-knowledge observability constraints and generates configurable hidden compatibility structure. The environment records policy-agnostic measures of task efficiency and coordination quality. In a scenario with 9 drones, 27 targets, and a hidden three-mode compatibility structure, the proposed policy reduces episode length by 45% relative to a random baseline, closes approximately 60% of the match-quality gap to an oracle benchmark, and finishes within 11% of oracle efficiency while discovering embedding structure aligned with the hidden drone-target compatibility modes. The results show that meaningful latent structure and useful decentralized coordination can emerge under strict zero-knowledge constraints, while also revealing characteristic limits of the setting, including crowding and overkill driven by the absence of explicit coordination and HP-aware allocation.
