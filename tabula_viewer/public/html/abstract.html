Abstract

Multi-Robot Task Allocation (MRTA) traditionally assumes that agents possess some prior knowledge of task properties, their own capabilities, or the capabilities of other agents, and often relies on explicit inter-agent communication. These assumptions limit the applicability of existing approaches in uncertain, decentralized, and communication-constrained environments. This work introduces Zero-Knowledge Multi-Robot Task Allocation (ZK-MRTA), a new problem formulation in which heterogeneous agents must allocate tasks without prior knowledge of task attributes, self-capabilities, or the capabilities of others, and without direct communication. Under this formulation, agents can learn only from the outcomes of their own actions and from indirect observation of the environment.

To address this setting, this paper proposes a decentralized collaborative-filtering-inspired approach based on matrix factorization. The core idea is to reinterpret collaborative filtering not as preference prediction, but as latent utility estimation: agents infer hidden task suitability from sparse interaction outcomes collected over time. Each agent maintains a local latent model and independently selects tasks using estimated utilities, while coordination emerges indirectly through shared public observations rather than explicit parameter exchange.

To support rigorous evaluation, the paper also presents a simulation benchmark for ZK-MRTA implemented as a Gymnasium-compatible PettingZoo parallel multi-agent environment. The framework enables comparison between decentralized strategies under controlled zero-knowledge assumptions and supports policy-agnostic performance metrics such as task completion efficiency, collision rate, overkill, and damage efficiency. The main contribution of this work is therefore threefold: the formalization of the ZK-MRTA problem, the introduction of a collaborative-filtering-based decentralized solution family, and the definition of an experimental benchmark for systematic analysis of task-allocation strategies in hidden, communication-free environments.
