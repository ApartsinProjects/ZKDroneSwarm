# 9. Conclusion

This paper introduced Zero-Knowledge Multi-Robot Task Allocation (ZK-MRTA) as a formally defined problem class and presented the first evaluation of a decentralized learning policy within this framework. The central contribution is the formalization itself: ZK-MRTA distinguishes a regime of task allocation problems in which agent-task compatibility is latent, unobservable, and must be inferred entirely from the outcomes of prior interactions. This formulation captures a class of coordination problems that existing MRTA and multi-agent RL frameworks do not address directly, and it provides a principled basis for designing and evaluating policies that operate under strict information constraints.

The proposed decentralized matrix-factorization policy adapts the collaborative filtering paradigm to this setting. Each agent independently maintains a local factorization of the drone-target interaction space, updated online from publicly observable interaction outcomes. There is no centralized model, no inter-agent communication, and no access to the hidden compatibility structure. The results demonstrate that this mechanism is sufficient to recover meaningful latent structure from ZK-constrained experience.

Across 35 training episodes, the policy reduced episode duration by 45% relative to the random baseline and converged to within 11% of the oracle on efficiency metrics, without any access to the privileged state the oracle requires. Average match quality improved by 174% over the training horizon, and the policy's internal embedding geometry organized itself into three distinct clusters aligned with the three ground-truth latent modes — a geometric signature of latent structure recovery that emerged without any explicit structural supervision.

The evaluation also surfaced two phenomena that are structural properties of decentralized learning under ZK constraints rather than incidental artifacts: an overkill paradox, in which improving match quality necessarily increases wasted damage due to the unavailability of HP state; and a crowding phenomenon, in which agents converge on shared utility estimates before differentiating their individual preferences, temporarily limiting coordination efficiency. Both phenomena are explained as consequences of the information constraint, not failures of the learning mechanism.

The current evaluation is a proof of concept rather than a definitive characterization. The policy was undertrained at the end of the 35-episode horizon, only one supervision mode and one swarm composition were evaluated, and no statistical replication was performed. These are well-defined gaps that the existing framework is directly suited to address.

ZK-MRTA as a problem class remains largely unexplored. The formalization presented here provides a foundation for systematic investigation of how latent structure, observability constraints, swarm composition, and learning architecture jointly determine what is achievable in decentralized task allocation without prior knowledge. The results suggest that the answer, even under strict constraints, is more than might be expected.
